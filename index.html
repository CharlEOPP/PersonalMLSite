I'm an undergraduate researcher in Computer Science with a focus on applied machine learning. My work aims to mix deep learning, time series analysis, and environmental science, with an emphasis on improving the efficiency and accuracy of climate simulations.

Climate models are some of the most computationally intensive simulations in science. My research explores whether data-driven approaches can capture the nonlinear dynamics of Earth's climate systems at a lower computational cost, and how ML models can be best integrated with existing physics-based frameworks.

I work with publicly available datasets from NASA and NOAA, building preprocessing pipelines and training neural networks to match and extend the predictive power of traditionally used solvers.

Research

Machine Learning to Improve
Climate Modeling

Undergraduate Research ML · Climate Science NASA · NOAA Datasets

Physics-based climate models have been the cornerstone of climate prediction for decades, but they are costly to simulate, these global simulations can take days to weeks on high-performance clusters. This project intends to find whether machine learning can serve as a high-efficiency helper, replicating and extending the predictive performance of traditional solvers.

Training on various datasets of global surface temperature anomalies, atmospheric CO₂ concentrations, and ocean circulation indices, multiple ML architectures are evaluated against physics-based benchmarks. Results demonstrate that neural networks achieve lower prediction error, capture nonlinear cross-variable dependencies, and reduce inference time much better, giving a strong argument for hybrid ML–physics modeling frameworks.

⚙ Methodology

Four models were trained and evaluated: a regularized linear regression baseline, gradient-boosted trees, a fully-connected feedforward network, and an LSTM-based recurrent architecture optimized for sequential climate data. Each was compared against a physics-based baseline derived from energy balance equations.

Linear Regression with L2 regularization — interpretable baseline, captures linear forcing relationships.
Gradient Boosted Trees (XGBoost) — ensemble method handling nonlinear feature interactions without manual feature engineering.
Feedforward Neural Network (3-layer MLP) — learned representations of multivariate climate state vectors.
LSTM Recurrent Network — explicitly models temporal dependencies and long-range autocorrelation in climate time series.
Physics-Based Baseline (EBM) — simplified energy balance model, computationally expensive but interpretable.

All ML models were evaluated on held-out test data using mean absolute error (MAE), root mean square error (RMSE), and coefficient of determination (R²). Inference time was benchmarked on identical hardware to allow direct comparison with the physics-based baseline.

⬡ Key Takeaways
  • Neural networks outperform physics-based baselines on prediction accuracy on held-out climate data.
  • ML models capture complex nonlinear interactions between temperature, CO₂, and ocean circulation indices that simpler physical models cannot represent without extensive parameterization.
  • Inference time is reduced by approximately 10×.
  • ML models exhibit better alignment to observed temperature trajectories.
  • A hybrid ML–physics architecture is the most promising direction: using ML as a fast replacement for computationally expensive subcomponents while preserving physical constraints in the outer loop.

◎ Conclusion

This project demonstrates that machine learning offers a viable, and efficient, complement to traditional climate simulation. The improvements in prediction accuracy and inference speed represent a qualitative shift in what's computationally manageable for ensemble forecasting and real-time analysis.

The most exciting direction forward is the integration of ML into hybrid model architectures, where physics-based equations constrain the high-level dynamics and data-driven modules handle computationally expensive subprocesses. The combination of these preserves the interpretability and physical grounding of traditional models while also getting the speed and pattern-recognition capacity of neural networks.
